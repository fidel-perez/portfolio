Infrastructure & networking

  • Raspberry Pi as always-on gateway + Ubuntu server on-demand via Wake-on-LAN
  • Headscale (self-hosted Tailscale control plane) for secure mesh VPN across all devices
  • Caddy reverse proxy with automatic HTTPS, dynamic config generation, and a "lazy service" pattern — containers auto-wake on first HTTP request
  • FreeDNS dynamic DNS updates every 15 min for the public endpoint
  • IPC mechanism: file-based trigger + LAN broadcast (UDP) to wake and orchestrate the server from the Pi

CI/CD & configuration

  • Ansible playbooks managing both nodes with roles: common, pi_server, plex_server, ubuntu_desktop
  • Cron jobs, Docker setup, configs, SSH, and systemd units all templated via Jinja2
  • Git webhook listener (Flask) receives GitHub webhooks, triggers docker compose build && up
  • Everything version-controlled in one monorepo

Data & backups

  • Centralized JSON store (custom containerized service) with git-based auto-commit + push every minute
  • Nightly cloud backups to pCloud + Google Drive via rclone, with log aggregation and Telegram notifications
  • Docker system prune scheduled weekly for cleanup
  • Healthcheck.io integration for uptime monitoring of all critical services

Bots & automation

  • Telegram bot (serverbot): server start/stop, backup triggers, task reminders, Ring intercom integration, unified command system
  • ntfy for push notifications across devices

AI / LLM stack

AI-first engineering vision

The bottleneck in engineering has shifted. It's no longer writing code — it's the criteria behind what gets built, how it's architected, and whether the output is sound. This is where engineering is going, and I've already been working this way for over a year.

The shift

Workflows, team structures, and communication layers were designed for a world where writing code was the bottleneck. It's not anymore. The bottleneck is now criteria: knowing what to build, recognizing when the AI's output is wrong, and translating stakeholder needs directly into technical direction.

The opportunity

Companies that restructure workflows around AI-assisted development — not just “giving AI to workers” — can become dramatically more dynamic. When AI writes the code in minutes and the test suite alongside it, you need fewer handoffs and more technically sharp people who understand the business need, guide the AI, validate the output, and ship.

What I bring

11+ years of platform engineering, self-taught from civil engineering, with a proven track record of applying strong judgment to AI-assisted workflows. I generate multiple architectural options, evaluate tradeoffs, and catch when solutions are heading in the wrong direction — that's the skill that matters now.

Hard questions, honest answers
If we put you in front of a whiteboard, what happens?
I'd do exactly what I'd do on the job — ask clarifying questions to understand the constraints, then use AI to generate two or three architectural options, and walk you through why I'd pick one over the others. If you want to test whether I can evaluate architecture, that's a great test. If you want to test whether I can draw boxes from memory without tools, that tests something I don't think matters anymore. I'm happy to do a live exercise with AI access — that's how the actual work gets done.
How do we know you actually understand the systems and aren't just parroting AI?
My 11 years of hands-on engineering are what let me catch when AI is wrong. I can read Scala, Python, SQL, Terraform — I've written all of them professionally. The difference is I don't insist on writing them by hand when a tool can do it faster under my supervision. Give me a broken pipeline or a flawed architecture and I'll tell you what's wrong with it — that's the skill that matters.
Every candidate says they use AI. What makes you different?
Most engineers use AI to autocomplete code or ask questions. I use it to generate entire solutions, evaluate multiple approaches, build test suites, and deploy. The difference is that I've restructured my entire workflow around it — not bolted it onto the old one. I reach my Claude and Gemini subscription limits almost daily. I've built my own self-hosted AI communication tools. This isn't a feature I use, it's how I work.
You're asking us to change our interview process. Why should we?
I'm not asking you to lower the bar — I'm asking you to test the right thing. If your engineers will be working with AI daily, testing them without AI is like testing a pilot without a cockpit. I'm happy to be evaluated rigorously, but on criteria, judgment, and results — not on memorization.
What if the AI hallucinates something critical in production?
That's exactly why this role needs an experienced engineer, not just a prompt writer. I enforce automated test suites, I review critical commits manually, I always ask the AI to summarize what it did and why — which gives me early warnings. And I apply a fail-fast-fix-quick methodology. The risk of AI errors exists, but it's manageable with discipline. The risk of ignoring this shift entirely is much bigger.
You say departments don't make sense anymore. Can you be specific?
In a traditional setup you have product managers translating for developers, QA teams testing what developers wrote, DevOps teams deploying what QA approved. When AI writes the code in minutes and the test suite alongside it, you don't need all those handoffs. You need a technically sharp person who understands the business need, guides the AI, validates the output, and ships. That's fewer layers, faster delivery, and fewer people doing busywork disguised as process.
Aren't you just describing a staff engineer role?
Partially, but staff engineers in most companies still operate within the traditional framework — they design, others implement. What I'm describing collapses that. The person with the criteria also directs the implementation through AI, reviews it, and ships it. It's closer to a technical lead in a very flat, very fast team.

What each phase actually looks like

AI-assisted
where most companies are
  • AI tools in engineers' hands. Same team structure. Same review chains. Same sprint cadence.
  • The work gets done faster — then waits. PMs still translate for developers. QA still reviews what AI already tested.
  • Documentation written for human onboarding instead of structured for agent consumption.
  • The productivity gain evaporates into process bottleneck.

Ask yourself

— What does your company offer that GPT won't do autonomously in 18 months?

— How many humans does a stakeholder request pass through before it becomes running code? Could that number be one?

— What percentage of your engineering output is already AI-generated? If you don't know — that's the answer.

AI-native
where forward companies are moving
  • Context as infrastructure. Your edge is business context models can't access without you — structured, versioned, machine-readable schemas for agents, not wikis for humans.
  • MCP server layers exposing proprietary domain knowledge as tool-callable endpoints. When a model can query your business rules in real time — that's a moat.
  • Evaluation frameworks testing AI output against business criteria before production, not after.
  • Model-agnostic orchestration. Your criteria layer is yours. The model is replaceable.
  • Stakeholder-to-deployment interfaces. One request becomes a deployed artifact with one technical human in the loop — not five.
  • Cost-aware compute routing. Knowing when a task needs a $0.002 call, a $0.06 call, or a human — an engineering problem most companies haven't framed yet.
